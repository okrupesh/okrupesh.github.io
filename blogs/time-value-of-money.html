Why $1 Today Is Not Worth $1 Tomorrow?

By Rupesh Dubey (MScFE Candidate) · Published Oct 19, 2025

Understanding the Core Idea: The Time Value of Money (TVM)

Have you ever wondered why one dollar today is not worth the same as one dollar tomorrow? This is the basic idea behind the Time Value of Money (TVM), a key concept in finance and financial engineering.

The reasoning is straightforward: money today can be invested to earn a return, so it is naturally worth more than the same amount received in the future. People also prefer having money now rather than later, unless they are compensated for waiting. That compensation comes in the form of interest rates, which represent the reward for saving or the cost of borrowing.

The Math Behind Present and Future Values

To compare money at different points in time, we need a way to move its value forward or backward. This is done using Present Value (PV) and Future Value (FV) calculations.

Simple Interest

Simple interest is calculated only on the original amount, known as the principal. If you invest an amount PV for n years at an annual rate r, the future value is:

$$ FV = PV \times (1 + nr) $$

This means your money grows in a straight line over time.

Compound Interest

Compound interest takes things further. Here, you earn interest not only on your initial investment but also on the interest it has already earned. This creates exponential growth, which is how most real-world investments work. The future value and present value formulas become:

$$ FV = PV \times (1 + r)^t $$

$$ PV = \frac{FV}{(1 + r)^t} $$

Continuous Compounding

In some cases, especially in theoretical finance, we assume interest is added continuously rather than at specific intervals. Using the mathematical constant e (approximately 2.718) gives us:

$$ FV = PV \times e^{rt} $$

Continuous compounding represents the idea of your money growing at every instant.

The Yield Curve: Reading the Market’s Mood

Interest rates change depending on how long you invest your money. If we plot the relationship between the rate of return (the yield) and the time until repayment (the maturity), we get what is called the Yield Curve.

What the Shape Tells Us

  • A normal yield curve slopes upward. This means long-term interest rates are higher than short-term ones. Investors expect to earn more for locking up their money for a longer time.
  • An inverted yield curve slopes downward. This means short-term rates are higher than long-term ones, which often signals that investors expect economic trouble ahead. Inverted yield curves have often been seen as early warnings of recessions.

Spot and Forward Rates

The spot interest rate, written as \( r(0, t) \), is the rate available today for an investment that matures at time \( t \). From spot rates, we can calculate a forward rate, written as \( f(0, s, t-s) \), which is the implied rate for a period that begins in the future (at time \( s \)).

The relationship between these rates is given by:

$$ f(0, s, t-s) = \left[ \frac{(1 + r(0, t))^t}{(1 + r(0, s))^s} \right]^{1/(t-s)} - 1 $$

Bringing It All Together

The Time Value of Money is more than just a formula; it is the foundation of how finance works. By understanding it, we can make better decisions about saving, borrowing, and investing.

Every financial model, from pricing bonds to valuing companies, depends on this idea: A dollar today is worth more than a dollar tomorrow.
