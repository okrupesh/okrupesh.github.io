Can You Really Get a "Free Lunch" in Finance? Why Portfolio Risk Is Non-Linear

By Rupesh Dubey (MScFE Candidate) · Published Oct 28, 2025

Portfolio Return: The Simple Weighted Average

When combining assets (say Stock A and Stock B) to form a portfolio, the expected return \( r_P \) is straightforward because it follows linearity. The portfolio’s expected return is simply the weighted average of the individual expected returns:

$$ r_P = w_A \times r_A + w_B \times r_B $$

Because of this linear relationship, the portfolio's expected return will always lie between the individual returns of the assets it holds.

Portfolio Volatility: The Key Is Non-Linearity

While portfolio return is linear, portfolio volatility (standard deviation, \( \sigma_P \)) is not. This non-linearity is what makes diversification powerful.

The full expression for portfolio variance \( \sigma_P^2 \) includes a crucial interaction term that captures how the assets move together:

$$ \sigma_P^2 = w_A^2 \sigma_A^2 + w_B^2 \sigma_B^2 + 2\rho_{A,B} w_A w_B \sigma_A \sigma_B $$

Here:

  • \( w_A, w_B \) are the weights of each asset in the portfolio,
  • \( \sigma_A, \sigma_B \) are the individual volatilities,
  • \( \rho_{A,B} \) is the correlation coefficient between the assets, ranging from \(-1\) to \(+1\).

This third term is what introduces non-linearity. It means the overall portfolio risk depends not only on how risky each asset is individually, but also on how their returns move together.

The Power of Diversification (The "Free Lunch")

Diversification is often called the only "free lunch" in finance because it allows investors to reduce overall volatility \( \sigma_P \) without necessarily giving up expected return \( r_P \).

Correlation (\( \rho \)) Effect on Portfolio Risk (\( \sigma_P \)) Financial Implication
\( \rho = +1.0 \) (perfect positive) Risk is maximized; no diversification benefit. Portfolio risk is simply the weighted average of individual risks.
\( \rho < +1.0 \) (less than perfect) Risk is reduced relative to the simple average. Any correlation less than 1 leads to a lower total variance.
\( \rho = -1.0 \) (perfect negative) Risk can be minimized dramatically. A perfect hedge: one asset’s loss is fully offset by the other’s gain.

When assets are less than perfectly correlated, investors benefit from variance reduction. Mathematically, this effect can be highlighted as:

$$ \text{Variance Reduction} = (1 - \rho_{A,B})(2 w_A w_B \sigma_A \sigma_B) $$

Since \( 1 - \rho_{A,B} \) drives the extent of diversification benefit, lower correlation means greater portfolio efficiency. In some cases, the portfolio volatility can even be lower than the volatility of either individual asset.

Conclusion

The move from analyzing a single asset to understanding an entire portfolio reveals the true complexity of risk. Portfolio risk is non-linear, driven by correlations rather than simple addition. By exploiting this principle, a financial engineer can design efficient portfolios that achieve the highest expected return for the least amount of volatility: the closest thing finance has to a real "free lunch."
