Why Can't We Trust That Stock Returns Are "Normal"?

By Rupesh Dubey (MScFE Candidate) · Published Oct 21, 2025

The Appeal of Normality (The Bell Curve)

In finance, one of the most convenient assumptions is that asset returns follow a Normal (Gaussian) distribution, often visualized as the smooth and symmetrical "bell curve."

This assumption is attractive because the entire distribution can be summarized by just two parameters:

  • The mean (μ): the expected or average return.
  • The standard deviation (σ): the expected distance from the mean, representing volatility.

If returns were truly normal, life would be much simpler. Portfolio construction, Value at Risk (VaR) estimation, and risk management could all rely on elegant closed-form equations. However, financial markets rarely behave this neatly. In reality, returns often violate the symmetry and shape that define the normal curve.

The First Violation: Asymmetry (Skewness)

The first major departure from normality is skewness, which measures asymmetry in the distribution of returns. A normal distribution is perfectly symmetric, meaning that gains and losses of equal size are equally likely.

Interpreting Skewness

  • Zero skewness: the distribution is symmetric, matching the ideal normal curve.
  • Positive skewness: the right tail is longer, creating a higher chance of very large, positive returns.
  • Negative skewness: the left tail is longer, signaling that large losses are more likely than a normal model would predict.

Many financial assets show negative skewness due to "panic days" when prices fall sharply. In such cases, the standard deviation alone no longer represents risk accurately. Using a symmetric model (like the normal distribution) will underestimate the likelihood and impact of extreme losses.

The Second Violation: Fat Tails (Kurtosis)

The second and often more dangerous violation is kurtosis, which measures how "peaked" or "flat" a distribution is compared to the normal curve.

The standard normal distribution has a kurtosis of 3. In finance, we often discuss excess kurtosis, which is kurtosis minus 3.

Interpreting Kurtosis

  • Zero excess kurtosis: the distribution matches the normal curve.
  • Positive excess kurtosis (leptokurtic): the distribution has "fat tails" and a more pronounced peak, meaning extreme outliers occur more frequently than the normal model predicts.

When excess kurtosis is positive, it indicates that volatility itself is unstable. Markets can shift suddenly from calm to chaos, and the usual assumption of a single, steady level of risk breaks down.

Instead of one consistent bell curve, real market returns may come from a mixture of different volatility states — a phenomenon often called "volatility of volatility."

The Student’s t-Distribution

One alternative that accounts for fat tails is the Student’s t-distribution. It naturally allows for heavier tails and is controlled by its degrees of freedom — fewer degrees mean fatter tails. This makes it better suited for modeling financial returns that experience occasional but extreme movements.

Conclusion: Why Advanced Modeling Matters

Assuming that stock returns are normal when they clearly are not is one of the fastest ways to create misleading financial models. Real markets show both skewness and excess kurtosis, meaning that extreme events are more likely than the bell curve suggests.

The role of financial engineers and risk managers is to recognize where simple models fail and adopt more realistic alternatives — such as the Student’s t-distribution or nonparametric approaches — that can capture tail risk, the risk of rare but devastating events.

Understanding these deviations from the bell curve is what separates theoretical models from real-world finance.
